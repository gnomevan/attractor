\section{Perturbation Sensitivity Analysis}

The perturbation scale $\varepsilon$ used in Lyapunov exponent computation must be chosen carefully. We performed a sensitivity sweep over $\varepsilon \in \{10^{-3}, 10^{-4}, 10^{-5}, 10^{-6}, 10^{-7}, 10^{-8}, 10^{-9}\}$ at five learning rates for the baseline MLP (14K parameters, synthetic data, 5 seeds each).

At $\varepsilon = 10^{-8}$ and below, the measured Lyapunov exponent is uniformly $\lambda \approx +0.00004$ per training step regardless of learning rate---a numerical artifact arising from finite-precision arithmetic rather than genuine dynamical sensitivity. Converged measurements require $\varepsilon \geq 10^{-6}$. All results in the main text use $\varepsilon = 10^{-5}$.

This finding has implications for prior work: Lyapunov exponent measurements at $\varepsilon = 10^{-8}$ or smaller should be interpreted with caution, as the reported positive exponents may reflect numerical noise rather than chaotic dynamics.

\section{Convergence of $D_2$ and $\lambda$ with Measurement Parameters}

We verified that the choices $\varepsilon = 10^{-5}$ and $N_\text{inputs} = 100$ used in the main text are within converged regimes. For the CNN on CIFAR-10 at 30\% of EoS (seed 0), we swept $N_\text{inputs} \in \{25, 50, 100, 200, 400\}$ and $\varepsilon \in \{10^{-4}, 5{\times}10^{-5}, 10^{-5}, 5{\times}10^{-6}, 10^{-6}\}$.

The correlation dimension is stable: $D_2$ varies by only 0.10 across all $N_\text{inputs}$ values (range 3.74--3.83) and is completely independent of $\varepsilon$, as expected since $\varepsilon$ affects only the perturbation protocol, not the trajectory. The production value $D_2 = 3.74$ at $N_\text{inputs} = 100$ sits in the center of this range.

The Lyapunov exponent shows more structure: $\lambda$ varies by a factor of ${\sim}3$ across $\varepsilon$ values, with a dip at $\varepsilon = 10^{-5}$. All values remain positive ($\lambda \in [0.8, 2.3] \times 10^{-4}$ per step), confirming chaos across the full $\varepsilon$ range tested. The variation with $\varepsilon$ is consistent across all $N_\text{inputs}$ values, indicating a genuine scale-dependent feature of the dynamics rather than a numerical artifact. Crucially, the qualitative conclusion---positive Lyapunov exponent at 30\% of EoS---is robust to the choice of $\varepsilon$ within $[10^{-6}, 10^{-4}]$.

\section{Batch-Size Sweep: Robustness Under SGD}

The main text reports all measurements under full-batch gradient descent. To test whether the strange attractor survives stochastic gradient noise, we sweep batch size $B \in \{2000, 1000, 500, 200, 100\}$ for the CNN/CIFAR-10 condition at 30\% of EoS, using a paired-noise protocol: at each training step $t$, a deterministic index generator (seeded by $\text{seed} \times 10^5 + t$) selects the mini-batch, and both the original and perturbed model copies train on the identical subset. This ensures the Lyapunov exponent measurement isolates deterministic sensitivity from stochastic forcing---the standard paired-noise technique from stochastic dynamical systems. The EoS threshold is measured once at full-batch ($B = 2000$) and the learning rate is held fixed across all batch sizes; only the gradient noise level changes.

The crossover batch size differs between architectures ($B \approx 500$ for the CNN, $B \approx 200$ for the MLP), consistent with the MLP at 90\% EoS having stronger underlying deterministic dynamics that tolerate more noise before the signal degrades.

\section{Stationarity of the Measurement Window}

The $D_2$ measurements discard the first 20\% of each trajectory as transient. However, as Fig.~1(d) of the main text shows, the top Hessian eigenvalue at 95\% of EoS is still rising at step 5{,}000, raising the question of whether the dynamics have reached a stationary regime at the highest learning rates.

At the learning rates where the main quantitative claims are made (CNN at 30\% EoS, MLPs at 90\% EoS), the sharpness dynamics show bounded oscillation rather than secular drift, consistent with a quasi-stationary regime within the measurement window. At 95\% EoS, where sharpness is clearly non-stationary, the measured $D_2$ should be interpreted as characterizing the quasi-stationary dynamics of the approach to equilibrium rather than an asymptotic attractor. We note that this caveat applies primarily to the highest EoS fractions (90--95\%), which are not the conditions on which our main claims rest; the CNN peak at 30\% EoS and the MLP convergence analysis at 90\% EoS both show stable sharpness within the measurement window.

A windowed analysis---computing $D_2$ separately over the first and second halves of the post-transient trajectory---would provide a direct test of stationarity; we leave this for future work.

\section{Embedding Dimension}

The Grassberger-Procaccia algorithm is applied in a function-space embedding. The trajectory points are the network's output vectors on a fixed set of held-out inputs, giving a natural embedding in $\mathbb{R}^{N_\text{inputs} \times N_\text{classes}}$ (here $100 \times 10 = 1000$ dimensions). This high ambient dimension ensures that the embedding dimension far exceeds the measured $D_2 \approx 3$--$5$, satisfying the Whitney/Takens embedding criterion ($m > 2D_2$) without the need for time-delay embedding. The correlation integral $C(r)$ is computed from pairwise distances in this space, and the scaling exponent is extracted from the linear region of $\ln C(r)$ versus $\ln r$. Because the embedding dimension is fixed and much larger than $D_2$, we do not perform a $D_2(m)$ convergence sweep---this is unnecessary when the embedding is already sufficient. The PCA reduction to 10 dimensions used for persistent homology is applied only to the TDA analysis, not to the $D_2$ computation.
